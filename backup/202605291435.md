## 1. Abatacept versus hydroxychloroquine for prevention of rheumatoid arthritis in individuals with palindromic rheumatism: a randomized open-label trial (PALABA trial)

**Nature Medicine** | 2026 | DOI: [10.1038/s41591-026-04395-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04395-6)

**Metrics**: Citations (as of May 29 2026): 0 (very recent) | Journal IF: 50.0 (2024 JCR, Q1 — #1 in Medicine, Research & Experimental) | Altmetric: Not available via public API (new publication)

### Population
70 seropositive (RF+ and/or ACPA+) adults with recent-onset palindromic rheumatism across 14 Spanish centers. Patients at high risk for progression to classifiable rheumatoid arthritis.

### Method
Randomized open-label multicenter trial (NCT03669367). Subcutaneous abatacept (125 mg weekly for year 1, then every 2 weeks for year 2) versus hydroxychloroquine (5 mg/kg/day) for up to 24 months. Primary endpoint: progression to classifiable RA. Secondary outcomes included attack intensity and symptom remission.

### Case Number
n = 70 (34 abatacept, 36 hydroxychloroquine).

### Results
Progression to RA occurred in 7/34 (20.6%) on abatacept versus 18/36 (50%) on HCQ (P = 0.010; HR ≈ 0.27). Abatacept also significantly improved symptom remission rates (~56% vs ~23%) and reduced palindromic attack intensity. Both treatments were well tolerated with no major safety differences. Autoantibody titers did not change significantly in either arm.

### Conclusions
Abatacept significantly reduces the risk of progression to rheumatoid arthritis compared with hydroxychloroquine in seropositive patients with palindromic rheumatism and is well tolerated.

### Innovations
First randomized trial specifically testing T-cell costimulation blockade (abatacept) for RA prevention in the palindromic rheumatism population, extending prior work on pre-RA intervention strategies.

### Clinical Importance
Provides the first RCT evidence supporting a preventive strategy in a very early at-risk group. If confirmed, this could shift rheumatology practice toward earlier intervention to prevent RA onset rather than treating established disease.

## 2. Repurposing leflunomide and hydroxychloroquine to treat Sjögren's disease (RepurpSS-II): a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase 2b trial

**The Lancet Rheumatology** | 2026 (online 20 May) | DOI: [10.1016/S2665-9913(26)00075-5](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2665-9913(26)00075-5)

**Metrics**: Citations (as of May 29 2026): 1 | Journal IF: 16.4 (2025 JCR, Q1 — ranked #3 in rheumatology) | Altmetric: Not available via public API

### Population
Adults aged 18–75 years with primary Sjögren’s disease and active systemic involvement (ESSDAI ≥5). Single center (University Medical Center Utrecht, Netherlands). Predominantly female (≈93%), median age ≈55 years.

### Method
Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 2b trial. Leflunomide 20 mg + hydroxychloroquine 400 mg daily versus placebo for 24 weeks. Primary endpoint: between-group difference in change in ESSDAI score (mixed-effects model). Registered EudraCT 2020-001933-11. Patient representatives contributed to design.

### Case Number
n = 46 (21 leflunomide + HCQ, 25 placebo).

### Results
Primary endpoint met: mean difference in ESSDAI change at week 24 was –4.135 points (95% CI –6.558 to –1.709; p = 0.0012) favoring active treatment. Significant improvements in serum IgG, rheumatoid factor, and C4 levels. Higher composite responder rates (STAR: 64% vs 19%, p=0.007; CRESS: 57% vs 24%, p=0.046). No significant between-group differences in objective dryness measures or ESSPRI. The combination was generally well tolerated (GI discomfort most common); one unrelated serious AE in active arm.

### Conclusions
Leflunomide plus hydroxychloroquine is effective and safe for patients with systemically active Sjögren’s disease and replicates findings from the earlier RepurpSS-I trial.

### Innovations
Successful repurposing of two inexpensive, widely available conventional DMARDs in a disease area that has seen many negative trials for newer targeted therapies. Demonstrates reproducible efficacy of the combination.

### Clinical Importance
Offers a low-cost, accessible systemic treatment option for Sjögren’s disease where approved targeted therapies remain limited and expensive. Particularly relevant for healthcare systems prioritizing affordable care.

## 3. Vunakizumab for Radiographic Axial Spondyloarthritis: A Randomized Clinical Trial

**JAMA Network Open** | 2026 (online 8 May) | DOI: [10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2026.11632](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2026.11632)

**Metrics**: Citations (as of May 29 2026): 0 (very recent) | Journal IF: ≈10–12 (2024/2025 JCR, Q1 in general/internal medicine) | Altmetric: Not available via public API

### Population
548 adults with active radiographic axial spondyloarthritis (r-axSpA) in China. Mean age ≈33 years, ≈80% male.

### Method
Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 2/3 trial. Vunakizumab (novel IL-17A inhibitor) 120 mg versus placebo. Primary endpoints included ASAS20 and ASAS40 response at week 16, with follow-up through week 32. Evaluated rapid onset, sustained efficacy, and safety.

### Case Number
n = 548 (main comparison: 294 vunakizumab 120 mg, 146 placebo).

### Results
ASAS40 at week 16: 46.3% (136/294) with vunakizumab 120 mg vs 24.0% (35/146) with placebo (difference 22.3%, 95% CI 13.3–31.3; p < 0.001; NNT ≈4). ASAS20: 65.6% vs 42.5%. Rapid separation observed by weeks 2–4. Responses sustained; ASAS40 reached ≈62% by week 32 in continuing patients. Additional improvements in BASDAI, BASFI, BASMI, CRP, enthesitis, and quality-of-life measures. Safety profile consistent with other IL-17A inhibitors (AE rates ≈83.7% vs 81.5% placebo).

### Conclusions
Vunakizumab 120 mg significantly improves signs and symptoms of active radiographic axial spondyloarthritis with rapid onset and an acceptable safety profile.

### Innovations
New IL-17A inhibitor (AnDaJing) developed in China demonstrating robust efficacy in r-axSpA, adding to the maturing IL-17 pathway inhibitor class (including bimekizumab, secukinumab, ixekizumab).

### Clinical Importance
Expands therapeutic options for axial spondyloarthritis. The large effect size (NNT ≈4 for ASAS40) and rapid onset are clinically meaningful, especially for patients with inadequate response to prior therapies.
